Why I Started a Blog

May 22 2020

I recently read this article[1] about why motivation is overrated. A common theme in many self-help productivity books is to “find your motivation” and the actions will come easy afterwards. Yet motivation is just a feeling that pushes you to act a certain way. A feeling. Why rely on something as unreliable as a wave of feeling that passes by?

My most valuable takeaways from this article are:

"Motivation, broadly speaking, operates on the erroneous assumption that a particular mental or emotional state is necessary to complete a task."

"Discipline, by contrast, separates outwards functioning from moods and feelings and thereby ironically circumvents the problem by consistently improving them."

"In summary, motivation is trying to feel like doing stuff. Discipline is doing it even if you don’t feel like it."


There is a huge mindset shift that lies in the quote above. If you reprogram your mind to rely on discipline rather than motivation, you will get many more things done. With motivation, you feel good before you start doing the task. With discipline, you feel great after you finish. The latter is arguably the superior choice.

Now, this isn’t meant to denounce the value that lies in the occasional spurt of motivation that you feel. By all means, take advantage of the sudden drive when it comes your way and use it to pump out some work. However, don’t rely on the erratic pattern of motivation as your only source of fuel to get work done.

A great way to start implementing this discipline-based mindset into your own lives is to write out to-do lists and exercise your discipline muscle in order to complete them each day. Don’t overestimate your abilities and write ten thousand to-dos or else you may experience burnout. Respect your deadlines and don’t use the excuse of “I’ll do it tomorrow.” After all, if you can’t respect your own deadlines, why should anyone else?

References:
1. "Screw motivation, what you need is discipline." by Widsomination
